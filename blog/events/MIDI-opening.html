MIDI opening

MIDI opening

Feb 19, 2017. | By: Emil Orvik Kollstrøm

February 15th 2017 the Department of Computer Science (IDI) at NTNU’s very own Makerspace had its grand opening. Projects from Makerspace at IDI (MIDI) were presented by the Makerspace’s developer team, visions for the space’s future were communicated and food was consumed.

Part of the audience

Why is MIDI needed?

Professor Pekka Abrahamsson - MIDI’s visionary and head of operations - welcomed everybody and spoke of the intentions and visions for the future of MIDI. He spoke of MIDI’s role as a makerspace for all members of the department (IDI), including students, professors and PhD candidates, and MIDI’s job of spreading maker-culture and enabling for physical product design. Abrahamsson also expressed his vision of making MIDI the leading lab for safe and secure construction of cyber-physical systems, and to collaborate with leading European companies and research institues.

Developer Sigve Skaugvoll showcasing the highstriker t-shirt

After welcoming everybody to the event Abrahamsson went on to introduce Professor and Head of Department Maria Letizia Jaccheri. She expressed her enthusiasm for Professor Abrahamsson’s initiative and made it clear that the department as a whole gave its full support for the project.

Professor Martin Steinert from the Department of Mechanical and Industrial Engineering was the next speaker to take the floor. Steinert passionately emphasized the importance of actually making ideas and theories into physical entities, and not only rely on simulations to prove that they work:

If you have an idea: try it out and make it physically! If you do you’ll uncover a whole range of things that you couldn’t even begin to imagine at the idea-stage of the project.

The team behind the invinsible drone presenting their project

The first student to take the podium at the event was project manager of Vortex NTNU Peter Aaser. Vortex is a student organization at NTNU composed of students from different engineering disciplines. Each year they design and build a remotely operated vehicle (ROV) from scratch to compete in the MATE international ROV competition. Aaser talked about the cooperation between Vortex and MIDI and how the two can benefit from their partnership going forward. He talked about the valuable experience Vortex gained from competing in MATE and the stressing situations they had encountered when only days before the competition their ROV was not functional. Experience that he hopes that the MIDI community can learn and have use for as well. Vortex will have access to MIDI’s 3D-printers that will allow them to perform rapid prototyping of essential system components.

Project presentations

The projects that the developers at MIDI had been working on before the opening were then presented. Four projects were presented:

  1. Magic Mirror: the smart mirror that lets you use your mirror as a computer without your hands
  2. Retro Arcade: over 1700 games in one arcade for free
  3. Invinsible drone: the most inexpensive drone with 3D-printed parts
  4. Tomorrow’s T-shirt: embedded clothing with the highstriker game in it

Håvard Estensen with the Magic Mirror

After the presentations the food arrived and the attendees were served pizza and sodas and were able to mingle.
